🦣 Woolly Mammoths

Woolly mammoths were large animals that looked similar to modern elephants and once lived in large numbers across North America, Europe and Asia. However, they became extinct around 4,000 years ago.

Scientists believe they became extinct for a number of reasons. The first is that their natural habitat decreased in size as the world became warmer. The second reason is that humans hunted and killed them for food, clothing and shelter, and also used their bones and teeth to create some basic tools, and used their fur and their shape to make art.

We know quite a lot about mammoths because some of their bodies have been found frozen in the ice in places like Siberia. Scientists have examined the stomachs of some mammoths and found the grass and plants that were still inside, which tells us what they ate. By studying mammoth skeletons, scientists have also been able to compare the bodies of mammoths with those of modern elephants. Their ears were short compared to an African or Asian elephant's ears, which helped them to stay warm. Their teeth were also different.

Some scientists are now trying to recreate mammoths by taking DNA from the frozen bodies and creating a new mammoth, which is a possibility. However, this is controversial. Some scientists believe it is better to give the new mammoth a certain amount of freedom. Others worry that mammoths and modern elephants were both social animals and that a 'new' mammoth would be lonely. There is also the possibility that a 'new' mammoth might develop an 'old' disease that no longer exists in the modern world.

🦡 The Honey Badger

The honey badger is one of the most fascinating and unusual creatures in the animal kingdom. Although it is not a big animal – a fully-grown honey badger is about 30 centimetres tall and typically weighs between 7 and 16 kilograms – it is also one of its most aggressive and tough. The honey badger is famous for being fearless; it will attack animals far bigger than itself if it feels threatened. Even humans are not safe from a honey badger if it thinks they will attack or harm it.

They are instantly recognizable by the grey and white stripes that run along the back of their mainly black body. This pattern and these colors are thought to warn other animals that they are dangerous.

The honey badger has an extremely varied diet. While they prefer to eat meat, they also eat eggs, roots, berries and even bee larvae. Small creatures such as beetles, lizards and birds form the biggest part of their diet. They locate their prey mainly using their excellent sense of smell.

They get their name from their love of honey, and they can destroy a beehive very easily. Beekeepers often set traps for honey badgers to prevent them from destroying beehives. For this type of food, the honey badger has a working relationship with another creature – the honeyguide bird. The honeyguide bird uses its calls to lead the honey badger to the beehive, and then shares the honey and bee larvae with the honey badger after it has broken open the hive.
